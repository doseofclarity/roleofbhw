Barangay Health Worker
The Backbone of Community Health

“Trained community health volunteers – first contact, trusted partner, health advocate.”

Definition & Legal Bases

Barangay Health Workers (BHWs) are trained community health workers or health auxiliary volunteers, including traditional birth attendants and healers. They form the village or barangay level of primary health care workers. (Source: PHN White Book, Chapter II – Primary Health Care)

Key Legal Bases:
• LOI 949 (1979) – adopted PHC in the Philippines.
• RA 7883 (Barangay Health Worker's Benefits and Incentives Act of 1995) – provides benefits and incentives.
• RA 7305 (Magna Carta for Public Health Workers) – extends certain protections.
BHW as PHC worker: The PHC team consists of physician, nurse, midwife, sanitary inspector, and BHWs. BHWs are trained to assume redefined roles and functions.

Qualifications & Training

  • Resident of the barangay, preferably chosen by the community.
  • At least elementary level graduate; ability to read and write.
  • Willing to render volunteer service.
  • Training provided by DOH, LGU, or NGOs – basic health care, first aid, maternal & child health, immunization support, disease surveillance, community organizing.
  • After training, BHWs are accredited by the LGU and become members of the Barangay Health Committee.
Refresher courses and continuing education are provided to update skills (e.g., new vaccines, IMCI, CDD).

Roles & Responsibilities of BHWs

Based on the PHN White Book (Chapter II, IV, IX) and DOH guidelines, BHWs perform:

  • Health promotion & education: Conduct community assemblies, mother's classes, one-on-one counseling.
  • First aid & emergency response: Basic first aid, referral of emergencies.
  • Maternal & child health: Assist in prenatal promotion, accompany pregnant women to RHU, promote breastfeeding, growth monitoring.
  • Immunization support: Mobilize children for EPI, assist in vaccination sessions, maintain target client lists.
  • Family planning: Distribute pills/condoms (after training), counsel on FP methods, refer for IUD/BTL.
  • Communicable disease control: Case finding, DOTS support (TB), distribute ORS for diarrhea, environmental sanitation promotion.
  • Nutrition: Identify malnourished children, assist in feeding programs, promote vegetable gardens.
  • Record keeping: Maintain family folders, report vital events (births, deaths), submit reports to RHU.

BHW in Specific Public Health Programs

Expanded Program on Immunization (EPI): BHWs help identify infants for vaccination, inform parents of schedules, and support outreach immunization days (Reaching Every Barangay strategy).
TB-DOTS: BHWs can serve as treatment partners (DOT providers) under supervision of PHN or midwife – observe patients taking anti-TB drugs.
Family Planning: BHWs are trained to provide pills and condoms, counsel on FP, and refer for other methods.
Control of Diarrheal Diseases (CDD): BHWs teach ORS preparation, promote handwashing, and refer severe cases.
Malaria & Filariasis: BHWs assist in case finding, blood smear collection (with training), and distribution of insecticide-treated nets.
Nutrition Program: BHWs weigh children, identify underweight, refer to feeding programs, and promote micronutrient supplementation.

BHW as Part of the PHC Team

The Primary Health Care team is interdisciplinary. BHWs are the frontline link between the formal health system and the community.

  • Team composition: Physician, PHN, Midwife, Sanitary Inspector, BHW, and at times dentist, nutritionist.
  • BHW role in the team: Community mobilization, early detection, basic care, referral, follow-up.
  • BHWs attend regular team meetings at the RHU and participate in planning health activities.
  • They are supervised by the Public Health Nurse or Rural Health Midwife.
  • In the Integrated Management of Childhood Illness (IMCI) community component, BHWs are trained to assess and classify common childhood illnesses and refer appropriately.

Benefits & Incentives (RA 7883)

Republic Act 7883 – Barangay Health Worker's Benefits and Incentives Act of 1995 provides:

  • Monthly honorarium (amount determined by LGU).
  • Access to skills training and continuing education.
  • Free medical and dental services in government facilities.
  • Priority in selection for government health-related employment.
  • Insurance coverage (accident/injury while on duty).
  • Recognition and awards for outstanding performance.
LGU responsibility: Local governments are mandated to accredit BHWs, provide supplies, and ensure their integration into the local health system.

PHN-BHW Collaboration

The Public Health Nurse works closely with BHWs through:

  • Training & capacity building: PHNs conduct orientation and refresher courses for BHWs.
  • Supervision & support: Regular BHW meetings, home visit accompaniment, review of records.
  • Referral coordination: BHWs refer patients to the RHU; PHNs ensure appropriate follow-up.
  • Community assemblies: PHNs and BHWs jointly organize health education sessions.
  • Data collection: BHWs assist in surveys, masterlisting, and reporting (FHSIS target client lists).

Devolved setting: BHWs are under the supervision of the LGU but receive technical guidance from the PHN and RHU staff.
